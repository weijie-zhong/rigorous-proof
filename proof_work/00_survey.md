# Literature Survey — General Premium-Family Bundle Dominance

## 1. Problem Classification

### Primary area
**Mechanism Design / Optimal Auction Theory** — specifically, multi-item
monopoly pricing (screening) with a single buyer whose type is
multidimensional.

### Sub-areas (in order of relevance)

1. **Optimal transport and Kantorovich duality in mechanism design.**
   The proof's core verification tool is the DDT saddle-point criterion, which
   recasts revenue maximization as a transport problem with a one-sided cost.
   The sufficiency argument constructs a transport plan (coupling) satisfying
   specific marginal and cost-equality constraints.

2. **Lattice theory and combinatorial order theory.**
   The premium family $\mathcal{F}$ is an upper set (filter) in the Boolean
   lattice $(2^{[N]} \setminus \{\emptyset\}, \subseteq)$. The cone dominance
   lemma exploits the upward-closure property, and the antichain
   $\mathcal{A} = \min(\mathcal{F})$ organizes the combinatorial structure.
   The partial order $\preceq_{B_k}$ on type space is a coordinate-flipped
   product order.

3. **Multivariate dependence theory (MTP2 / total positivity).**
   The density hypotheses (H9, H10) involve multivariate totally positive
   distributions of order 2 (MTP2), which connect to log-supermodularity,
   positive association, and the FKG inequality. The proof requires conditional
   versions via Karlin--Rinott.

4. **Convex analysis and polyhedral geometry.**
   Mechanisms are piecewise-linear convex functions on polyhedral partitions.
   Direction cones $K^{\mathcal{F}}_{B_k}$ are polyhedral cones whose facial
   structure encodes the dominance relations among bundles.

5. **Measure theory and disintegration.**
   The signed DDT measure $\mu$ has a point mass, surface measures on boundary
   faces, and an absolutely continuous interior part. The proof disintegrates
   along level sets of the cell sum $s_k$.

## 2. Key Objects and Structures

### 2.1 The premium family $\mathcal{F}$ and its combinatorial structure

**Object:** $\mathcal{F} \subseteq 2^{[N]} \setminus \{\emptyset\}$, an upper
set in the inclusion poset.

**Properties that matter:**
- **Upward closure:** $B \in \mathcal{F}$, $B \subseteq B' \implies B' \in \mathcal{F}$.
  This is the *only* combinatorial property used in the cone dominance lemma.
- **Antichain of generators:** $\mathcal{A} = \min(\mathcal{F})$ is an antichain
  in $(2^{[N]}, \subseteq)$, and $\mathcal{F}$ is the order filter generated
  by $\mathcal{A}$. The converse (Part II(b)) uses the structure of $\mathcal{A}$
  to identify items $j \notin \bigcap \mathcal{A}$.
- **Relation to principal filters:** The single-core case $\mathcal{F} = \{B : C \subseteq B\}$
  is the principal filter generated by $\{C\}$ (i.e., $\mathcal{A} = \{C\}$).
  The general case allows $|\mathcal{A}| > 1$.

**Subtlety:** When $|\mathcal{A}| > 1$, different minimal elements of $\mathcal{F}$
may be incomparable under inclusion. The cone $K^{\mathcal{F}}_{B_k}$ is defined
with respect to *all* of $\mathcal{F}$, not just the generators, so the cone
characterization is more nuanced than in the principal-filter case.

### 2.2 Direction cones $K^{\mathcal{F}}_{B_k}$

**Object:** Polyhedral cone in $\mathbb{R}^N$ defined by $\sum_{B_k} h_i \ge \sum_{B'} h_i$
for all $B' \in \mathcal{F}$ and $\sum_{B_k} h_i \ge 0$.

**Properties that matter:**
- In the single-core case, the cone admits an explicit three-part characterization:
  $h_j \le 0$ for $j \notin B_k$, $h_l \ge 0$ for $l \in B_k \setminus C$,
  $\sum_{B_k} h_i \ge 0$. The general upper-set case yields a *weaker*
  characterization: the cone dominance lemma shows $h_j \le 0$ for $j \notin B_k$,
  but the sign of $h_l$ for $l \in B_k$ is not individually constrained (only
  the aggregate $\sum_{B_k} h_i \ge 0$ holds).
- The positive-displacement bound $S_+(h) \le \varepsilon_k(x^2)$ follows from
  $h_i \le \bar{v}_i - x^2_i$ for $i \in B_k$ when $h = x^1 - x^2$ with both
  points in $D$.

### 2.3 The partial order $\preceq_{B_k}$ and the lattice $(D, \preceq_{B_k})$

**Object:** $x \preceq_{B_k} x'$ iff $x_i \le x'_i$ for $i \in B_k$ and
$x_j \ge x'_j$ for $j \notin B_k$.

**Properties that matter:**
- $(D, \preceq_{B_k})$ is a distributive lattice with coordinate-wise meet/join
  (with appropriate flipping for non-$B_k$ coordinates).
- MTP2 and the radial score monotonicity are stated with respect to this order.
- The selling cells $C_k$ are upper sets under $\preceq_{B_k}$ (Lemma 3 in the
  core-bundle proof), which is needed for the EPW conditional-association step.

### 2.4 The DDT signed measure $\mu$

**Object:** $\mu = \delta_0 + \mu^\partial - \mu^-$ on $D$, arising from
integration by parts of the revenue functional.

**Properties that matter:**
- $\mu(D) = 0$ (total mass zero).
- The positive part $\mu^+$ has a point mass at the origin and surface measures
  on top faces.
- The negative part $\mu^-$ has density $w(x) = \nabla f \cdot x + (N+1)f(x) > 0$
  on $\operatorname{int}(D)$.
- Cell mass balance $\nu^+(C_k) = \nu^-(C_k)$ follows from restricted optimality.

### 2.5 The piecewise-linear mechanism $M$

**Object:** Convex function on $D$ with slope $q^{B_k}$ on cell $C_k$ and
slope $0$ on $C_0$.

**Properties that matter:**
- Slopes satisfy $\mathbf{1}^\top q^{B_k} = 1$ (full allocation within bundle
  $B_k$). The slope $q^{B_k}$ has support on the coordinates of $B_k$.
- The partition $\{C_k\}$ is determined by the prices of the bundles in the
  restricted problem.

### 2.6 Boundary/interior tails and the crossing point

**Objects:** $B_k(q) = \nu^+(C_k \cap \{s_k \ge q\})$,
$I_k(q) = \nu^-(C_k \cap \{s_k \ge q\})$, and $p_k = \sup\{q : B_k(q) > I_k(q)\}$.

**Properties that matter:**
- The tail dominance $B_k(q) > I_k(q)$ for $q > p_k$ is needed for the
  monotone-coupling step in the shifted-tail argument.
- The inclusivity condition $p_k < s_*^k$ ensures that boundary mass
  concentrates above the crossing point, enabling the obliqueness bound.

## 3. Hidden Assumptions

### 3.1 Type space is a closed box $D = \prod [0, \bar{v}_i]$
The entire framework (signed measure, boundary faces, etc.) depends on $D$
being a rectangular box with each coordinate bounded in $[0, \bar{v}_i]$.
The divergence theorem application and the top-face surface measures are
specific to this geometry. This rules out:
- Unbounded type spaces (e.g., exponential types).
- Non-rectangular type spaces (e.g., simplices).
- Discrete type spaces.

### 3.2 Linear-in-type valuations
Valuations are $v_B(x) = \alpha^{B} \sum_{i \in B} x_i$ where $\alpha^B$
depends only on whether $B \in \mathcal{F}$. This is additive across items
within each bundle (no within-bundle complementarities beyond the
$\alpha$-premium). The theory does not apply to non-linear valuations
$v_B(x) = g_B(x_{B})$ for general $g_B$.

### 3.3 Binary premium structure
Each bundle is either fully premium ($\alpha$-amplified) or fully non-premium
(additive). There is no intermediate amplification. This simplifies the cost
function to a two-family maximum.

### 3.4 Buyer-types are drawn from a *single* density
The framework assumes a single-buyer monopoly screening problem. There is no
competition among buyers (no auction), no common values, and no outside option
beyond the zero payoff.

### 3.5 Regularity: $f \in C^2(D)$ and $f > 0$
- $f > 0$ everywhere on $D$ (including the boundary) is used to ensure:
  (a) $\mu^\partial$ has full support on top faces,
  (b) conditional densities given $s_k = s$ exist and are well-defined,
  (c) the radial score $\Phi(x)$ is well-defined everywhere.
- $C^2$ smoothness is used for the MTP2 characterization via mixed partials
  of $\log f$, and for differentiability of $\Phi$.

### 3.6 $\mu^-$ is purely interior
The virtual positivity condition $w(x) > 0$ on $\operatorname{int}(D)$ ensures
that all negative mass of $\mu$ is in the strict interior. If $w$ vanished on
an interior set, the transport construction would need to handle zero-density
regions.

### 3.7 Piecewise-linear optimality in the restricted problem
The restricted-optimal mechanism $M$ is assumed piecewise-linear. This is not
a generic feature of optimal mechanisms — it requires specific structure of
the feasible region and cost function. The DDT framework guarantees existence
of piecewise-linear optima in the class of problems considered, but this is
itself a non-trivial result.

### 3.8 Cell mass balance
The condition $\nu^+(C_k) = \nu^-(C_k)$ for each selling cell is stated as a
consequence of restricted optimality but is itself a non-trivial property. It
follows from the saddle-point criterion applied cell-by-cell.

### 3.9 Items are *distinguishable* within bundles
The setup treats $v_B(x) = \alpha \sum_{i \in B} x_i$, not $v_B(x) = \alpha \cdot |B| \cdot g(\bar{x}_B)$
or any symmetric reduction. Each item contributes its own valuation coordinate.

### 3.10 The exclusion cell is a single connected region
The proof treats $C_0$ as the complement of the selling cells in $D$. No
explicit assumption requires $C_0$ to be connected, but the structure of
piecewise-linear mechanisms typically gives $C_0$ a convex (hence connected)
shape near the origin.

## 4. Directly Applicable Theorems

### Theorem 1: Saddle-Point Criterion (Daskalakis--Deckelbaum--Tzamos, 2017)

**Source:** Daskalakis, C., Deckelbaum, A., and Tzamos, C. "Strong Duality
for a Multiple-Good Monopolist." *Econometrica*, 85(3):735–767, 2017.

**Full statement:** Let $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^N$ be a compact convex set,
$f$ a density on $D$, and $\mu$ the associated signed measure on $D$. Let
$u: D \to \mathbb{R}_+$ be a piecewise-linear convex function with cells
$\{C_k\}_{k=0}^K$ and slopes $\{q^k\}_{k=0}^K$ with $q^k \in \mathcal{Q}$.
Let $c: D \times D \to \mathbb{R}_+$ be the dual transport cost
$c(y,y') = \max_{q \in \mathcal{Q}} q \cdot (y - y')$.

If there exists a non-negative measure $\gamma$ on $D \times D$ such that:
1. (Cell compatibility) $\gamma(C_k \times C_k^c) = 0$ for every $k$.
2. (Cost equality) For $\gamma$-a.e. $(y,y')$ in $C_k \times C_k$:
   $q^k \cdot (y - y') = c(y,y')$.
3. (Marginal identity) $\gamma_1 - \gamma_2 = \mu$.

Then $u$ is an optimal mechanism and $\gamma$ is an optimal transport plan.

**How it applies:** This is the *verification* tool. The entire sufficiency
proof (Part I) constructs $\gamma$ satisfying these three conditions for the
full cost $c_\alpha$. The restricted optimality (H4) provides a $\gamma^{\mathcal{F}}$
satisfying these conditions for the restricted cost $c^{\mathcal{F}}_\alpha$.

**Hypotheses to check:**
- Compactness of $D$: satisfied (closed box).
- Convexity of $\mathcal{Q}$: satisfied (allocation simplex).
- The cost function must match the allocation constraint set $\mathcal{Q}$.
  For the full problem, $\mathcal{Q}$ allows allocation to all $2^N - 1$
  bundles, so $c_\alpha(h) = \max(0, \max_B v_B(h))$.

### Theorem 2: Strassen's Theorem (Strassen, 1965)

**Source:** Strassen, V. "The Existence of Probability Measures with Given
Marginals." *Annals of Mathematical Statistics*, 36(2):423–439, 1965.

**Full statement:** Let $(X, \le)$ be a partially ordered Polish space with
the order relation closed. Let $\mu_1, \mu_2$ be Borel probability measures
on $X$ with equal total mass. A coupling $\pi$ of $\mu_1$ and $\mu_2$
supported on $\{(x,y) : x \ge y\}$ exists if and only if for every bounded
measurable function $\phi: X \to \mathbb{R}$ that is increasing (i.e.,
$x \ge y \implies \phi(x) \ge \phi(y)$):
$$\int \phi \, d\mu_1 \ge \int \phi \, d\mu_2.$$

**How it applies:** Used in Layer 2 to construct the cell-level oblique
transport $\gamma_k$ supported on $\{(x^1, x^2) : x^1 \succeq_{B_k} x^2\}$.
The stochastic dominance condition (increasing functions have larger
$\nu^+|_{C_k}$-integral than $\nu^-|_{C_k}$-integral) is verified using the
MTP2 and decreasing-$\Phi$ hypotheses.

**Hypotheses to check:**
- $(D, \preceq_{B_k})$ is a partially ordered Polish space: $D$ is a compact
  metric space (Polish), and the order $\preceq_{B_k}$ is closed in $D \times D$
  (defined by weak inequalities). Satisfied.
- Equal total mass: $\nu^+(C_k) = \nu^-(C_k)$. Satisfied by cell mass balance.
- Increasing-function integrability: bounded measurable functions on a compact
  space are integrable. Satisfied.

### Theorem 3: Monotone Coupling Lemma (Univariate)

**Source:** Standard; see e.g. Lindvall (2002), *Lectures on the Coupling
Method*, or Thorisson (2000), *Coupling, Stationarity, and Regeneration*.

**Full statement:** Let $\alpha, \beta$ be finite Borel measures on
$\mathbb{R}$ with $\alpha(\mathbb{R}) = \beta(\mathbb{R})$. If
$\alpha([r, \infty)) \ge \beta([r, \infty))$ for all $r \in \mathbb{R}$,
then there exists a coupling $\pi$ of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ supported on
$\{(x,y) : x \ge y\}$.

**How it applies:** Used in the shifted-tail argument. After disintegrating
along $s_k$, the tail dominance (H7) provides the stochastic dominance
needed for a monotone coupling of the $s_k$-marginals of $\nu^+|_{C_k}$ and
$\nu^-|_{C_k}$.

**Hypotheses to check:**
- Equal total mass of $s_k$-pushforwards: follows from cell mass balance.
- Tail dominance: provided by (H7).

### Theorem 4: Karlin--Rinott Conditional MTP2 Theorem (Karlin--Rinott, 1980)

**Source:** Karlin, S. and Rinott, Y. "Classes of Orderings of Measures and
Related Correlation Inequalities. I. Multivariate Totally Positive
Distributions." *Journal of Multivariate Analysis*, 10(4):467–498, 1980.

**Full statement (relevant parts):**

Let $X = (X_1, \ldots, X_N)$ have an MTP2 density $f$ on a lattice
$L \subseteq \mathbb{R}^N$. Let $T = \sum_{i=1}^N a_i X_i$ with all
$a_i > 0$. Then:

(a) **Conditional MTP2:** For each fixed value $t$ of $T$, the conditional
density $f(x \mid T = t)$ is MTP2 on the slice $\{x : \sum a_i x_i = t\} \cap L$.

(b) **TP2 kernel:** For any bounded measurable increasing function $\phi$,
the conditional expectation $E[\phi(X) \mid T = t]$ is TP2 as a function of $t$
(i.e., increasing in $t$).

(c) **Positive association:** MTP2 implies positive association: for any two
increasing functions $\phi, \psi$, $\text{Cov}(\phi(X), \psi(X)) \ge 0$.

**How it applies:**
- Part (a) is used after disintegrating $f|_{C_k}$ along
  $s_k = \sum_{i \in B_k} x_i$: the conditional density on the slice is MTP2.
- Part (b) provides the monotonicity needed to verify stochastic dominance
  conditions for Strassen's theorem.
- Part (c) is used via the FKG inequality to bound covariances.

**Hypotheses to check:**
- MTP2 density: provided by (H9).
- Positive coefficients: $s_k = \sum_{i \in B_k} x_i$ uses coefficients
  $a_i = 1$ for $i \in B_k$. But coordinates $j \notin B_k$ are *absent*
  from $s_k$, not weighted by positive coefficients. The extension to partial
  sums (listed in §7 of the distilled problem) handles this via
  $\varepsilon$-perturbation.

**Critical caveat:** Karlin--Rinott requires *all* coefficients positive.
The cell sum $s_k = \sum_{i \in B_k} x_i$ uses only a subset of coordinates.
The extension technique perturbs to $T_\varepsilon = s_k + \varepsilon \sum_{j \notin B_k} (\bar{v}_j - x_j)$,
which has all positive coefficients under $\preceq_{B_k}$ (the flipped
coordinates make the non-$B_k$ terms positive-coefficient). Then
$T_\varepsilon \to s_k$ as $\varepsilon \to 0$.

### Theorem 5: FKG Inequality (Fortuin--Kasteleyn--Ginibre, 1971)

**Source:** Fortuin, C. M., Kasteleyn, P. W., and Ginibre, J. "Correlation
Inequalities on Some Partially Ordered Sets." *Communications in Mathematical
Physics*, 22(2):89–103, 1971.

**Full statement:** Let $\mu$ be a probability measure on a finite
distributive lattice $L$ satisfying the lattice condition (FKG condition):
$\mu(x \vee y) \mu(x \wedge y) \ge \mu(x) \mu(y)$ for all $x, y \in L$.
Then for any two increasing functions $\phi, \psi: L \to \mathbb{R}$:
$$\int \phi \psi \, d\mu \ge \left(\int \phi \, d\mu\right) \left(\int \psi \, d\mu\right).$$

**Continuous version:** For a continuous density $f$ on a box
$\prod [a_i, b_i]$ that is MTP2 (log-supermodular), the same inequality holds
for bounded measurable increasing functions.

**How it applies:** Used to verify that increasing functions have
non-negative covariance under the conditional distributions in the
cell-level transport construction. Specifically, after conditioning on
$\{X \in C_k, s_k = s\}$, the MTP2 property (preserved by Karlin--Rinott
conditional theorem and EPW) gives positive association, and FKG converts
this to the covariance bound needed.

**Hypotheses to check:**
- MTP2 / log-supermodularity: provided by (H9) and preserved by conditioning.
- Functions must be increasing w.r.t. $\preceq_{B_k}$: must verify for each
  specific function used in the proof.

### Theorem 6: Esary--Proschan--Walkup Conditional Association (1967)

**Source:** Esary, J. D., Proschan, F., and Walkup, D. W. "Association of
Random Variables, with Applications." *Annals of Mathematical Statistics*,
38(5):1466–1474, 1967.

**Full statement:** If $X = (X_1, \ldots, X_N)$ is positively associated
(i.e., $\text{Cov}(\phi(X), \psi(X)) \ge 0$ for all pairs of coordinate-wise
increasing, bounded measurable functions $\phi, \psi$), and $A$ is an
increasing event (i.e., $x \in A$ and $x \le y$ implies $y \in A$), then
the conditional distribution $X \mid X \in A$ is also positively associated.

**How it applies:** The event $\{X \in C_k\}$ is an increasing event under
$\preceq_{B_k}$ (because $C_k$ is a $\preceq_{B_k}$-upper set, established
in Lemma 3 of the core-bundle proof). EPW then preserves positive association
of $f$ when conditioned on $C_k$.

**Hypotheses to check:**
- Positive association of $X$: follows from MTP2 (H9) by Karlin--Rinott (c).
- $\{X \in C_k\}$ is increasing under $\preceq_{B_k}$: follows from cell
  upper-set property (Lemma 3). **Must verify** that this lemma generalizes
  from the core-bundle case to the general upper-set case.

### Theorem 7: Disintegration Theorem

**Source:** Standard; see e.g. Ambrosio, Gigli, and Savaré (2008), *Gradient
Flows in Metric Spaces*, or Bogachev (2007), *Measure Theory*.

**Full statement:** Let $\mu$ be a finite Borel measure on a Polish space
$X$, and let $T: X \to Y$ be a measurable map into a Polish space $Y$. Then
there exists a family $\{\mu_y\}_{y \in Y}$ of probability measures on $X$
(the regular conditional distributions) such that:
1. For $T_\#\mu$-a.e. $y$, $\mu_y$ is supported on $T^{-1}(y)$.
2. $\mu = \int_Y \mu_y \, d(T_\#\mu)(y)$.

**How it applies:** Disintegrate $\nu^+|_{C_k}$ and $\nu^-|_{C_k}$ along
$s_k: D \to \mathbb{R}$. This yields conditional distributions on the slices
$\{x \in C_k : s_k(x) = s\}$, which are then coupled using the MTP2
machinery.

**Hypotheses to check:**
- $D$ is Polish: compact metric space, hence Polish. Satisfied.
- $s_k$ is measurable: continuous function, hence measurable. Satisfied.

## 5. Closely Related Results

### 5.1 Core-Bundle Dominance (predecessor theorem)

**Source:** The single-core case proved in `proof_work_v2/` and written up in
`core_bundle_dominance.md`.

**Setting:** $\mathcal{F} = \{B : C \subseteq B\}$ for a fixed core
$C \subseteq [N]$ (a principal filter).

**Key difference from the general case:** In the principal-filter case,
$|\mathcal{A}| = 1$, and the cone $K^C_{B_k}$ admits a tight three-part
characterization: $h_j \le 0$ for $j \notin B_k$, $h_l \ge 0$ for
$l \in B_k \setminus C$, $\sum_{B_k} h_i \ge 0$. In the general upper-set
case, only the first and third conditions survive: the cone dominance lemma
gives $h_j \le 0$ for $j \notin B_k$, but does *not* guarantee $h_l \ge 0$
for individual $l \in B_k$, because there may not exist $B' \in \mathcal{F}$
with $B' = B_k \setminus \{l\}$.

**What carries over:** Layers 2–5 of the proof (oblique transport, threshold,
boundary partition, saddle-point) are structurally identical. Layer 1 (cone
dominance) is the only layer that uses the specific form of $\mathcal{F}$.

### 5.2 DDT Strong Duality (Daskalakis--Deckelbaum--Tzamos, 2017)

**Source:** *Econometrica*, 85(3):735–767, 2017.

**Result:** In the multi-item monopoly screening problem with additive
valuations $v_B(x) = \sum_{i \in B} x_i$, optimal mechanisms can be found
by solving a transport dual. Strong duality holds, and optimal mechanisms are
piecewise-linear when the type distribution is "generic."

**Relevance:** Provides the framework (signed measure, cost function,
saddle-point criterion) within which the entire proof operates. However, DDT
treat *additive* valuations; the present problem has *selectively
superadditive* valuations (with the $\alpha$-premium for $\mathcal{F}$-bundles).

### 5.3 DDT "Mechanism Design via Optimal Transport" (2013/2024)

**Source:** Daskalakis, C., Deckelbaum, A., and Tzamos, C. "Mechanism Design
via Optimal Transport." *EC 2013*, extended version 2024.

**Result:** Establishes the transport duality for mechanism design with
general type spaces and valuation structures. Introduces the geometric
framework of "cells" and "cones" for piecewise-linear mechanisms.

**Relevance:** The conceptual foundation for Definitions 1, 8, and the
entire saddle-point approach.

### 5.4 Hart--Nisan (2019) — Approximate Revenue in Multi-Item Auctions

**Source:** Hart, S. and Nisan, N. "Selling Multiple Items."
*Journal of Economic Theory*, 176:4–40, 2019.

**Result:** For two items with i.i.d. values, the optimal mechanism may
involve randomized bundling. Grand bundling or separate selling can be
arbitrarily suboptimal relative to the optimal mechanism.

**Relevance:** Shows that bundle dominance (a restricted mechanism being
optimal for the full problem) is *not* a generic phenomenon — it requires
specific structural conditions. This motivates the strong hypotheses
(upper-set, MTP2, inclusivity) in the present theorem.

### 5.5 Manelli--Vincent (2006/2007) — Bundling as Optimal Mechanism

**Source:** Manelli, A. M. and Vincent, D. R. "Bundling as an Optimal
Selling Mechanism for a Multiple-Good Monopolist." *Journal of Economic
Theory*, 127(1):1–35, 2006.

**Result:** For two items, pure bundling is optimal under certain conditions
on the value distribution. Characterizes when grand bundling dominates all
other deterministic mechanisms.

**Relevance:** An early result on bundle dominance in two-item settings.
The present theorem generalizes this to $N$ items with selective
complementarities.

### 5.6 Rochet--Choné (1998) — Multidimensional Screening

**Source:** Rochet, J.-C. and Choné, P. "Ironing, Sweeping, and
Multidimensional Screening." *Econometrica*, 66(4):783–826, 1998.

**Result:** Solves the multidimensional screening problem in continuous type
spaces, identifying the role of "bunching" (multiple types receiving the same
allocation). Introduces the convex-analytic framework for multidimensional
mechanism design.

**Relevance:** Foundational for understanding why piecewise-linear mechanisms
arise and how the cell partition structure works. The "ironing" concept is
related to the boundary partition in Layer 4.

### 5.7 Haghpanah--Hartline (2021) — When Is Pure Bundling Optimal?

**Source:** Haghpanah, N. and Hartline, J. D. "When Is Pure Bundling
Optimal?" *Review of Economic Studies*, 88(3):1127–1156, 2021.

**Result:** Pure bundling is optimal in the two-item case under conditions
related to the "revenue-ordering" of items and log-concavity of the sum
distribution.

**Relevance:** The conditions in their theorem (which involve tail behavior
of the sum distribution) are related to the tail dominance condition (H7) in
the present theorem, though the settings are not identical.

### 5.8 Karlin--Rinott (1980) — Full Paper

**Source:** *Journal of Multivariate Analysis*, 10(4):467–498, 1980.

**Additional results beyond Theorem 4 above:**
- Theorem 2.1: Conditional density given a sufficient statistic of an
  exponential family is MTP2.
- Theorem 3.1: TP2 property of conditional expectations extends to
  multivariate increasing functions, not just coordinate projections.
- Theorem 4.1: MTP2 is preserved under marginalization.

**Relevance:** The extension to partial sums (conditioning on $s_k$ rather
than a full positive-coefficient linear combination) requires Theorem 3.1's
generalization and careful perturbation arguments.

### 5.9 Müller--Stoyan (2002) — Stochastic Orders

**Source:** Müller, A. and Stoyan, D. *Comparison Methods for Stochastic
Models and Risks.* Wiley, 2002.

**Relevant results:** Chapters on multivariate stochastic orders, especially
the connection between MTP2 densities and multivariate increasing convex
order. Strassen's theorem in the partially-ordered setting.

**Relevance:** Reference for the stochastic dominance conditions verified
in Layer 2.

## 6. Useful Lemmas and Inequalities

### 6.1 Divergence Theorem (for the DDT measure)

The signed measure $\mu$ arises from applying the divergence theorem to the
revenue functional. For a $C^1$ vector field $\mathbf{F}$ on a bounded
domain with piecewise-smooth boundary:
$$\int_D \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} \, dx = \int_{\partial D} \mathbf{F} \cdot \hat{n} \, d\sigma.$$
Applied with $\mathbf{F}(x) = x \cdot f(x)$ (the "radial" vector field
weighted by the density), this yields $\mu(D) = 0$.

### 6.2 Fubini--Tonelli Theorem

Used throughout for iterated integration and disintegration. Key application:
exchanging the order of integration when computing tail functions $B_k(q)$
and $I_k(q)$.

### 6.3 Dominated Convergence Theorem

Used in the $\varepsilon$-perturbation argument for extending Karlin--Rinott
to partial sums: the conditional densities $f(\cdot \mid T_\varepsilon = t)$
converge to $f(\cdot \mid s_k = s)$ as $\varepsilon \to 0$, and the MTP2
property passes through the limit.

### 6.4 Monotone Class Theorem

Potentially needed to extend the Strassen stochastic dominance condition
from a generating class of increasing functions to all bounded measurable
increasing functions.

### 6.5 Support Function of a Polyhedral Cone

For the direction cone $K^{\mathcal{F}}_{B_k}$, the support function
$\sigma_{K}(q) = \sup\{q \cdot h : h \in K\}$ characterizes the dual cone.
Used implicitly in verifying cost equality (condition 2 of saddle-point):
$q^{B_k} \cdot h = c_\alpha(h)$ when $h \in K^{\mathcal{F}}_{B_k}$ and
the cost equality holds.

### 6.6 Polarity / Duality of Cones

The dual cone $K^* = \{q : q \cdot h \ge 0 \; \forall h \in K\}$ and the
polar cone $K^\circ = -K^*$ appear implicitly in the verification that the
slope $q^{B_k}$ attains the maximum in the cost function on the cone
$K^{\mathcal{F}}_{B_k}$.

### 6.7 Birkhoff's Representation Theorem for Distributive Lattices

$(2^{[N]}, \subseteq)$ is a distributive lattice. Upper sets in this lattice
are uniquely determined by their antichain of minimal elements. This is the
basis for the $\mathcal{A} = \min(\mathcal{F})$ decomposition.

### 6.8 Layer Cake / Cavalieri's Principle

For computing the total mass of tail functions:
$\int_0^{\bar{s}_k} [B_k(q) - I_k(q)] \, dq = \int_{C_k} s_k(x) \, d(\nu^+ - \nu^-)(x)$.
Used in verifying that the tail dominance condition is consistent with
cell mass balance.

### 6.9 Envelope Theorem / Milgrom--Segal (2002)

For the perturbation arguments in Part II (necessity), the envelope theorem
ensures that small perturbations of the mechanism (adding a new bundle at a
carefully chosen price) produce revenue changes that can be computed to first
order.

### 6.10 Implicit Function Theorem

Used implicitly when the perturbation argument in Part II needs to show that
the boundary between cells shifts smoothly when a new bundle is introduced
at price $\bar{v}_j - \varepsilon$.

## 7. Known Counterexamples

### 7.1 Hart--Nisan: Bundling can be suboptimal

**Setting:** Two items, i.i.d. uniformly distributed on $\{1, 2\}$
(discrete) or specific continuous distributions.

**Result:** Grand bundling is not optimal; separate selling or mixed
mechanisms achieve higher revenue.

**What it shows:** Without the MTP2 and tail-dominance conditions, bundle
dominance fails. This confirms that hypotheses (H7)–(H10) are not vacuous.

### 7.2 Thanassoulis (2004): Splitting can dominate bundling

**Setting:** Asymmetric two-item monopoly screening.

**Result:** Selling items separately can strictly dominate pure bundling when
items have very different value ranges.

**What it shows:** The inclusivity condition (H5, H8) is load-bearing: when
$\bar{v}_j$ is very different across items, the top face of a "small" item
may fall in the exclusion region, violating inclusivity and allowing separate
selling to dominate.

### 7.3 Non-upper-set family: augmentation strictly improves revenue

**Setting:** $N = 3$, $C = \{1,2\}$, $\mathcal{F} = \{\{1,2\}, \{1,2,3\}\}$
but with $\{1,3\}$ excluded from $\mathcal{F}$. Then $\mathcal{F}$ is an
upper set (since $\{1,2\} \subseteq \{1,2,3\}$ and both are in $\mathcal{F}$).
Now consider instead $\mathcal{F}' = \{\{1,2\}\}$: this is NOT an upper set
because $\{1,2\} \in \mathcal{F}'$ but $\{1,2,3\} \notin \mathcal{F}'$.

**Result:** The converse (Part II(a)) predicts that for $\mathcal{F}'$, the
restricted-optimal mechanism cannot be full-optimal for any $\alpha$, because
one can augment with $\{1,2,3\}$ (a non-premium bundle) to capture additional
surplus from buyers with high $x_3$.

**What it shows:** The upper-set condition is tight: weakening it to any
non-upper-set family creates exploitable gaps.

### 7.4 Non-inclusivity: singleton augmentation improves revenue

**Setting:** $N = 3$, $\mathcal{F} = \{B : \{1,2\} \subseteq B\}$ (principal
filter with core $\{1,2\}$). Item 3 has $\bar{v}_3$ smaller than the crossing
point $p$, so $F_3 \cap C_0 \neq \emptyset$.

**Result:** The singleton $\{3\}$ is non-premium. Offering $\{3\}$ at price
$\bar{v}_3 - \varepsilon$ captures excluded types with high $x_3$, giving a
$\Theta(\varepsilon)$ revenue improvement.

**What it shows:** The inclusivity condition (H5) is necessary, not merely
sufficient. Without it, there exist "stranded" high-value types that can be
profitably served by non-premium singletons.

### 7.5 MTP2 failure: oblique transport does not exist

**Setting:** $N = 2$, $f(x_1, x_2) \propto \exp(\rho x_1 x_2)$ with $\rho < 0$
(negative dependence between items).

**Result:** When $\rho$ is sufficiently negative, the density is *not* MTP2,
and the boundary-mass distribution is not stochastically dominated by the
interior-mass distribution in the required sense. The Strassen coupling
with the correct marginals and cone support does not exist.

**What it shows:** The MTP2 condition (H9) is essential for the transport
construction. Negative dependence between bundle items and non-bundle items
reverses the stochastic ordering needed.

### 7.6 Radial score non-monotonicity: FKG step fails

**Setting:** Density $f$ that is MTP2 but whose radial score $\Phi$ is not
$\preceq_{B_k}$-decreasing (e.g., certain bimodal densities on $D$).

**Result:** The virtual density $w = f \cdot \Phi$ has a non-monotone ratio
with $f$, breaking the tail dominance that the FKG/covariance argument
requires. The shifted-tail inequality fails.

**What it shows:** The $\Phi$-decreasing condition (H10) is not implied by
MTP2 alone and is independently necessary for the oblique transport.

### 7.7 Failure of cell mass balance without restricted optimality

**Setting:** A mechanism $M$ that is *not* optimal for the restricted problem
but has the same cell structure.

**Result:** The marginal identity $\gamma_1 - \gamma_2 = \mu$ cannot be
satisfied with cell-compatible transport, because
$\nu^+(C_k) \neq \nu^-(C_k)$.

**What it shows:** Restricted optimality (H4) is needed to ensure the
transport plan is well-defined; it cannot be replaced by mere feasibility.

## 8. Structural Comparison: Single-Core vs. General Upper Set

| Feature | Single-core ($\mathcal{F} = \{B : C \subseteq B\}$) | General upper set |
|---------|------------------------------------------------------|-------------------|
| Generators | $\mathcal{A} = \{C\}$ (single generator) | $\mathcal{A}$ may have $|\mathcal{A}| > 1$ |
| Cone characterization | Three-part: $h_j \le 0$ ($j \notin B_k$), $h_l \ge 0$ ($l \in B_k \setminus C$), $\sum_{B_k} h \ge 0$ | Two-part: $h_j \le 0$ ($j \notin B_k$), $\sum_{B_k} h \ge 0$. No individual sign for $l \in B_k$. |
| Cone dominance mechanism | $B_k \setminus \{l\} \supseteq C$ for $l \in B_k \setminus C$ | $B_k \setminus \{l\}$ may or may not be in $\mathcal{F}$ |
| $S_+$ bound applicability | $S_+(h) = \sum_{i \in B_k, h_i > 0} h_i$; only core items can contribute negatively | Same formula, but the contributing set may differ |
| Part II(a) converse | Vacuous (principal filter is always upper set) | Non-trivial (non-upper-set families exist) |
| Part II(b) converse | $j \notin C$ with $\bar{v}_j < p$ | $j \notin \bigcap \mathcal{A}$ with $\bar{v}_j < p$ |
